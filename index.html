Speedy-Splat: Fast 3D Gaussian Splatting with Sparse Pixels and Sparse Primitives

Alex Hanson, Allen Tu, Geng Lin, Vasu Singla, Matthias Zwicker, Tom Goldstein
University of Maryland
Paper arXiv Code

Abstract

3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) is a recent 3D scene reconstruction technique that enables real-time rendering of novel views by modeling scenes as parametric point clouds of differentiable 3D Gaussians. However, its rendering speed and model size still present bottlenecks, especially in resource-constrained settings.

In this paper, we identify and address two key inefficiencies in 3D-GS, achieving substantial improvements in rendering speed, model size, and training time. First, we optimize the rendering pipeline to precisely localize Gaussians in the scene, boosting rendering speed without altering visual fidelity. Second, we introduce a novel pruning technique and integrate it into the training pipeline, significantly reducing model size and training time while further raising rendering speed.

Our Speedy-Splat approach combines these techniques to accelerate average rendering speed by a drastic 6.71× across scenes from the Mip-NeRF 360, Tanks & Temples, and Deep Blending datasets with 10.6× fewer primitives than 3D-GS.

Method

Two aspects primarily determine the rendering speed of 3D Gaussian Splatting: the number of Gaussians allocated to each pixel and the total number of Gaussians in the scene. Speedy-Splat introduces methods to addresses both.

Gaussians per Pixel

Gaussian splatting is a tile-based renderer allocating Gaussians to tiles of pixels by computing Gaussian-tile intersections. Current Gaussian-tile intersection is overly conservative and we provide two methods to to make this localization exact.

SnugBox

A threshold on \( \alpha_i(p) = \sigma_i g_i(p) \) determines Gaussian \( \mathcal{G}_i \)'s extent, where \( \sigma_i \) is \( \mathcal{G}_i \)'s opacity and \( g_i(p) \) is \( \mathcal{G}_i \)'s value at pixel \( p \) when projected to the image plane. This gives the exact extent as an ellipse. We generate the tight bounding box for this ellipse -- the SnugBox -- by computing the 4 points where the first derivative of the ellipse is zero. By dividing by tile size, rounding, and clipping to the image boundary we obtain the rectangular tile extent in constant time.

AccuTile

Extending SnugBox, we compute exact tile intersection by loop over rows or columns of the SnugBox tile extent -- whichever is smallest -- and identifying the exact tile extent in that row or column. This is done by finding the maximum and minimum points of the ellipse in the row or column; all tiles between these points intersect the ellipse. Our insight is that this can be computed efficiently, requiring only two points to be computed per row or column.

Total Number of Gaussians

Recent post-hoc pruning technique PUP 3D-GS shows that roughly 90% of Gaussians can be pruned from trained scenes while maintaining high visual fidelity. We identify a reparameterization of the PUP 3D-GS pruning score that makes it 36x more memory efficient, allowing us to integrate this pruning into the training pipeline.

PUP 3D-GS computes a pruning score \( U_i \) per Gaussian \( \mathcal{G}_i \) as the log determinant of the second order approximation of the reconstruction error with respect to that \( \mathcal{G}_i \)'s mean \( \mu_i \) and scaling \( s_i \) parameters: \[ U_i = \log \left| \nabla_{\mu_i,s_i}^2 L_2 \right| = \log \left| \sum_{\phi \in \mathcal{P}_{gt}} \nabla_{\mu_i,s_i} I_{\mathcal{G}}(\phi) \nabla_{\mu_i,s_i} I_{\mathcal{G}}(\phi)^T \right|, \] where \( \mathcal{P}_{gt} \) are all training camera poses and \( I_{\mathcal{G}}(\phi) \) is the rendered view for pose \( \phi \). This second order approximation is shown to be exact when the \(L_1 \) error over \( \mathcal{P}_{gt} \) is zero.

We update this score by computing it with respect to the projected Gaussian values \( g_i \) instead of \( \mu_i \) and \( s_i \): \[ \tilde{U}_i = \log \left| \sum_{\phi \in \mathcal{P}_{gt}} \nabla_{g_i} I_{\mathcal{G}}(\phi) \nabla_{g_i} I_{\mathcal{G}}(\phi)^T \right|. \] Because \( g_i \) is a scalar at each pixel and \( \log \) is monotonically increasing, we can simplify the score to: \[ \tilde{U}_i = \sum_{\phi \in \mathcal{P}_{gt}} \nabla_{g_i} I_{\mathcal{G}}(\phi)^2. \] Empirically we observe that the \( L_1 \) loss converges quickly during training, thus allowing us to deploy this pruning during the densification stage of training -- what we call Soft Pruning -- and after the densification stage of training -- what we call Hard Pruning.
